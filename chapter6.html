Chapter 6
Verb codes


6.1   Introduction

It is the verb of a clause layer that determines which constituents (if any, besides the subject) are required to make the clause structurally complete. The annotation marks these requirements with a verb code added to the tag of each verb. The system of verb codes extends the verb code system of the fourth edition of the Oxford Advanced Learner's Dictionary (OALD4; Cowie 1989). These verb codes:

(i)
partially establish word sense,
(ii)
disambiguate the subject contribution for passivised verbs,
(iii)
signal when adverbial elements are selected items of the clause,
(iv)
provide a succinct notation to assist cataloging and searching clause structure, and
(v)
offer an independent check on the validity of the clause annotation.

The chapter is organised as follows. Section 6.2 presents OALD4 verb codes.


6.2   OALD4 verb codes

An OALD4 verb code starts with a capital letter: L, I, T, C, or D. This capital letter signals how the verb belongs to one of the verb classes of Table 6.1. These verb classes set requirements for the structural level of the clause: the NUMBER of elements and their FUNCTION. An element with subject function is always required in a basic clause, so mentioned elements are additional to the subject.

Table 6.1: Capital letters L, I, T, C, and D

L LINKING verb is associated with a SUBJECT PREDICATIVE (-PRD2), an element which provides information about the subject of the clause.
I INTRANSITIVE verb is NOT associated with a PREDICATIVE or an OBJECT, although it may be associated with an ADVERBIAL, an element which tells us about time, place, manner, etc of the action of the verb.
T TRANSITIVE verb MONO-TRANSITIVE verb is associated with a DIRECT OBJECT (-OB1), an element which often refers to the person or thing affected by the action of the verb.
C COMPLEX-TRANSITIVE verb is associated with both a DIRECT OBJECT (-OB1) and an OBJECT PREDICATIVE (-PRD), an element which provides more information about the direct object. Note: in the code, a dot divides information about the realisation of the direct object from information about the realisation of the object predicative.
D DITRANSITIVE verb is associated with both a DIRECT OBJECT (-OB1) and an INDIRECT OBJECT (-OB2), an element which refers to a person who receives something or benefits from an action. Note: in the code, a dot divides information about the realisation of the direct object from information about the realisation of the indirect object.

    A verb code involves a capital letter from Table 6.1 possibly followed by lower case letters from Table 6.2, possibly separated by a dot (‘.’) character. These lower case letters represent information about the FORM of the phrases that are used to realise the required elements that are signalled by their capital letter. For example, [La] is the code to indicate clause structure where there is a linking verb + subject predicative that is an adjective phrase, and [Cn.a] is the code to indicate clause structure where there is a complex-transitive verb + direct object that is a noun phrase + object predicative that is an adjective phrase.

Table 6.2: Lower case letters a, n, p, pr, n/pr, n/a, t, f, w, g, and i

aadjective phrase
nnoun phrase
padverb particle
prpreposition phrase
n/prnoun phrase or preposition phrase
n/aas + noun phrase or adjective phrase
tnon-finite clause (to-infinitive) (IP-INF with internal TO)
fthat-clause (CP-THT)
wfinite or non-finite clause with wh (CP-QUE)
gnon-finite clause ({ing} form) (IP-PPL)
inon-finite clause (‘bare’ infinitive) (IP-INF without internal TO)

    6.5.1   Perfect construction

    The perfect form of a verb generally calls attention to the consequences of a prior event; for example, (6.78a) implies that he is still away, in contrast with (6.78b).

    (6.78)
    a.
    He has gone to lunch.
    b.
    He went to lunch.

    ‘Had gone to lunch’ takes a past time point (i.e. when we arrived) as its reference point and is another way of establishing time relations in a text. The perfect tense is formed by:

    • turning the verb into its past participle inflection
    • adding a form of the verb HAVE before it.

    The progressive (also known as the ‘continuous’) form of a verb generally describes events in progress.     6.5.3   Voice

    Table 6.40: Tags for marking a logical subject

    NP-LGSlogical subject noun phrase
    PP-LGSlogical subject preposition phrase

    The ACTIVE VOICE is the basic, unmarked form of the clause.

    The PASSIVE VOICE is the more marked form of the clause in which the -SBJ corresponds in meaning to an -OB1 or -OB2 (or very occasionally a -PRD) of a corresponding active clause.

    The sentence It was eaten by our dog is the passive of Our dog ate it. A passive is recognisable from:

    • the past participle form eaten
    • the normal object (it) turned into the subject
    • the normal subject (our dog) turned into an optional preposition phrase with by
    • the verb BE, or some other verb, such as GET.
