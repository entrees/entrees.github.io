Chapter 6
Catenative verbs and passive voice


6.1   Introduction

Chapter 7 will focus on MAIN VERBS, where the main verb is either the only verb of a clause or the last verb to occur in a sequence of verbs of the same clause. Verbs of a verb sequence that are prior to the main verb are called CATENATIVE VERBS, giving the main topic of this chapter. There is also an overview of passive voice.

    A catenative verb occurs at a clause layer of the annotation alongside a clause intermediate layer with the -CAT extension. This clause intermediate layer is the selected complement of the catenative verb. Full tags for -CAT layers are listed in Table 6.1. The full tag for a -CAT layer is determined by the form of the directly contained verb.

Table 6.1: Tags for marking a layer selected as the complement of a catenative verb

IP-INF-CATlayer with infinitive verb introduced by a catenative verb
IP-PPL-CATlayer with participle verb introduced by a catenative verb

    The annotation marks each catenative verb with a catenative verb code, and sections 6.2 to 6.7 will provide examples to illustrate all of the possible codes. Codes differ because they indicate the form of the -CAT marked complement following Table 6.2.

Table 6.2: Lower case letters e, g, i, and t

eparticipial clause ({en} form) (IP-PPL-CAT)
gparticipial clause ({ing} form) (IP-PPL-CAT)
inon-finite clause (‘bare’ infinitive) (IP-INF-CAT without internal TO)
tnon-finite clause (to-infinitive) (IP-INF-CAT with internal TO)

A complete verb code is reached when cat_V is followed by a lower case letter from Table 6.2:

  • [cat_Ve] (section 6.2),
  • [cat_Vg] (section 6.3),
  • [cat_Vi] (section 6.4), and
  • [cat_Vt] (section 6.5).

There are also catenative verbs that take codes with an added lowercase ‘p’ to indicate the required presence of an adverb particle in addition to the -CAT marked complement:

  • [cat_Vp.g] (section 6.6) and
  • [cat_Vp.t] (section 6.7).

    In addition to matching catenative verb code commitments, having intermediate -CAT layers provides the structural isolation needed for coordination, which is possible with any clause intermediate layer.     Passive voice is discussed in section 6.8. Passive voice is most easily recognised when arising with an instance of BE that is a catenative [cat_Ve] verb, or some other catenative [cat_Ve] verb that is not HAVE, such as GET. Finally, section 6.9 introduces some specialised verb codes from Hornby (1975) that select passive complements.

    The [cat_Ve] verbs appeared of (6.2) and grown of (6.3) bring about a passive interpretation for the past participle ({en}) verb of their -CAT complement. Most typically, it is [cat_Ve] BE that is used to create the environment for a passive -CAT complement to occur, discussed in section 6.8 below. Also, [cat_Ve] GET is another notable trigger for a passive.

    One exception where a [cat_Ve] verb doesn't lead to a passive interpretation is HAVE, e.g., seen as past tense had in (6.3). Instead, [cat_Ve] HAVE brings about a perfect construction, discussed next in section 6.2.1.

6.2.1   Perfect construction

Catenative [cat_Ve] HAVE is used to form the perfect construction. The perfect tense is formed by:

  • turning the verb into its past participle inflection
  • adding a form of the verb HAVE before it.

This places multiple verbs in sequence, giving an IP-PPL-CAT layer in the annotation, as seen in (6.4).

(6.4)
=N(" Mr. Yamada has gone to Tokyo . ")
"IP-MAT","NP-SBJ","N","Mr."
"IP-MAT","NP-SBJ","NPR","Yamada"
"IP-MAT","HVP;~cat_Ve","has"
"IP-MAT","IP-PPL-CAT","VVN","gone"
"IP-MAT","IP-PPL-CAT","PP-DIR","P-ROLE","to"
"IP-MAT","IP-PPL-CAT","PP-DIR","NP","NPR","Tokyo"
"IP-MAT","PU","."
"ID","1366_samples_kisonihongo@10,9"

The perfect form of a verb generally calls attention to the consequences of a prior event; for example, (6.4) implies that Mr. Yamada is still away, in contrast with (6.5).

(6.5)
Mr. Yamada went to Tokyo.

6.8   Passive voice

Table 6.3: Tags for marking a logical subject

NP-LGSlogical subject noun phrase
PP-LGSlogical subject preposition phrase

The ACTIVE VOICE is the basic, unmarked form of the clause.

The PASSIVE VOICE is the more marked form of the clause in which the -SBJ corresponds in meaning to an -OB1 or -OB2 (or very occasionally a -PRD) of a corresponding active clause.

Sentence (6.18) is the passive of Her boyfriend gave her a new outfit. A passive is recognisable from:

  • the past participle form gave
  • a normal object (her) turned into the subject (She)
  • the normal subject (her boyfriend) turned into an optional preposition phrase with by
  • the catenative [cat_Ve] verb BE, or some other catenative [cat_Ve] verb, such as GET.
