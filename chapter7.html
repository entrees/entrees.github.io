Chapter 7
More verb codes


7.1   Introduction

Chapter 6 was concerned with providing verb codes for main verbs. The main verb is either the only verb of a clause or the last verb to occur in a sequence of verbs of the same clause. Verbs of a verb sequence that are prior to the main verb are called catenative verbs. Section 7.2 is an overview of the codes available for catenative verbs.

    Passive voice is discussed in section 7.3. Passive voice is most easily recognised when arising with the catenative [cat_Ve] verb BE, or some other catenative [cat_Ve] verb, such as GET.

    Additional verb codes in section 7.4 support the occurrence of existential there.


7.2   Catenative verb codes

A catenative verb occurs at a clause layer of the annotation alongside a clause intermediate layer with the -CAT extension. This clause intermediate layer is the selected complement of the catenative verb. Full tags for -CAT layers are listed in Table 7.1. The full tag for a -CAT layer is determined by the form of the directly contained verb.

    The remainder of this section illustrates the codes available for catenative verbs. Codes differ because they indicate the form of the -CAT marked complement:

  • [cat_Ve] (section 7.2.1),
  • [cat_Vg] (section 7.2.2),
  • [cat_Vi] (section 7.2.3), and
  • [cat_Vt] (section 7.2.4).

There are also catenative verbs that, in addition to a -CAT marked complement, select a particle as part of a phrasal verb:

  • [cat_phr_Vp.g] (section 7.2.5) and
  • [cat_phr_Vp.t] (section 7.2.6).

    The [cat_Ve] verbs appeared of (7.2) and grown of (7.3) bring about a passive interpretation for the past participle ({en}) verb of their -CAT complement. Most typically, it is [cat_Ve] BE that is used to create the environment for a passive -CAT complement to occur, discussed in section 7.3 below. Also, [cat_Ve] GET is another notable trigger for a passive.

    One exception where a [cat_Ve] verb doesn't lead to a passive interpretation is HAVE, e.g., seen as past tense had in (7.3). Instead, [cat_Ve] HAVE brings about a perfect construction, discussed next in section 7.2.1.1.

7.2.1.1   Perfect construction

Catenative [cat_Ve] HAVE is used to form the perfect construction. The perfect tense is formed by:

  • turning the verb into its past participle inflection
  • adding a form of the verb HAVE before it.

This places multiple verbs in sequence, giving an IP-PPL-CAT layer in the annotation, as seen in (7.4).

(7.4)
=N(" Mr. Yamada has gone to Tokyo . ")
"IP-MAT","NP-SBJ","N","Mr."
"IP-MAT","NP-SBJ","NPR","Yamada"
"IP-MAT","HVP;~cat_Ve","has"
"IP-MAT","IP-PPL-CAT","VVN","gone"
"IP-MAT","IP-PPL-CAT","PP-DIR","P-ROLE","to"
"IP-MAT","IP-PPL-CAT","PP-DIR","NP","NPR","Tokyo"
"IP-MAT","PU","."
"ID","1366_samples_kisonihongo@10,9"

The perfect form of a verb generally calls attention to the consequences of a prior event; for example, (7.4) implies that Mr. Yamada is still away, in contrast with (7.5).

(7.5)
Mr. Yamada went to Tokyo.

7.3   Passive voice

Table 7.2: Tags for marking a logical subject

NP-LGSlogical subject noun phrase
PP-LGSlogical subject preposition phrase

The ACTIVE VOICE is the basic, unmarked form of the clause.

The PASSIVE VOICE is the more marked form of the clause in which the -SBJ corresponds in meaning to an -OB1 or -OB2 (or very occasionally a -PRD) of a corresponding active clause.

Sentence (7.18) is the passive of Her boyfriend gave her a new outfit. A passive is recognisable from:

  • the past participle form gave
  • a normal object (her) turned into the subject (She)
  • the normal subject (her boyfriend) turned into an optional preposition phrase with by
  • the catenative [cat_Ve] verb BE, or some other catenative [cat_Ve] verb, such as GET.

7.4.1   [ex_V]

[ex_V] is the code to mark a verb that co-occurs with existential there with selection of an existential subject (-ESBJ) and nothing else. Example (7.25) illustrates how co-occuring there can be separated by interveing catenative verbs from the [ex_V] marked verb be with its existential subject trouble.

(7.25)
=N(" there <apos>s going to be trouble ")
"IML","EX","there"
"IML","BEP;~cat_Vg","<apos>s"
"IML","IP-PPL-CAT","VAG;~cat_Vt","going"
"IML","IP-PPL-CAT","IP-INF-CAT","TO","to"
"IML","IP-PPL-CAT","IP-INF-CAT","BE;~ex_V","be"
"IML","IP-PPL-CAT","IP-INF-CAT","NP-ESBJ","N","trouble"
"ID","20#587_christine_T33@12,14,2"

7.4.2   [ex_Va]

[ex_Va] is the code to mark a verb that co-occurs with existential there with selection of an existential subject (-ESBJ) + subject predicative that is an adjective phrase. Example (7.26) illustrates the [ex_Va] marked verb ai with selected existential subject nothing and subject predicative wrong with it.

(7.26)
=N(" there ai n<apos>t nothing wrong with it ")
"IP-MAT","EX","there"
"IP-MAT","VAI;~ex_Va","ai"
"IP-MAT","NEG","n<apos>t"
"IP-MAT","NP-ESBJ","Q","nothing"
"IP-MAT","ADJP-PRD2","ADJ","wrong"
"IP-MAT","ADJP-PRD2","PP","P-ROLE","with"
"IP-MAT","ADJP-PRD2","PP","NP","PRO","it"
"ID","72_christine_T14@4,8,11,2"

7.4.3   [ex_Vpr]

[ex_Vpr] is the code to mark a verb that co-occurs with existential there with selection of an existential subject (-ESBJ) + preposition phrase. In the annotation of (7.27), the selected preposition phrase is marked with location (-LOC) function.

(7.27)
=N(" There was an inscription on the door ")
"IP-MAT","EX","There"
"IP-MAT","BED;~ex_Vpr","was"
"IP-MAT","NP-ESBJ;{INSCRIPTION}","D","an"
"IP-MAT","NP-ESBJ;{INSCRIPTION}","N","inscription"
"IP-MAT","PP-LOC","P-ROLE","on"
"IP-MAT","PP-LOC","NP","D","the"
"IP-MAT","PP-LOC","NP","N","door"
"ID","437_classics_DICK_1952@4,6,11,2"

7.4.4   [ex_Vp]

[ex_Vp] is the code to mark a verb that co-occurs with existential there with selection of an existential subject (-ESBJ) + particle. In the annotation of (7.28), the selected particle is marked with location (-LOC) function.
