Chapter 7
More verb codes


7.1   Introduction

Chapter 6 was concerned with providing verb codes for main verbs. The main verb is either the only verb of a clause or the last verb to occur in a sequence of verbs of the same clause. Verbs of a verb sequence that are prior to the main verb are called catenative verbs.

    The initial focus of this chapter is to provide catenative verbs with verb codes. Section 7.2 is an overview of the codes available for catenative verbs. Section 7.3 presents options for clauses possibly with modal verbs and verb sequences involving HAVE and BE as catenative verbs. Additional codes of this chapter include specialised codes from Hornby (1975) in section 7.4; and codes for verbs that support the occurrence of existential there, section 7.5.

A catenative verb occurs at a clause layer of the annotation alongside a clause intermediate layer with the -CAT extension. This clause intermediate layer is the selected complement of the catenative verb. Full tags for -CAT layers are listed in Table 7.1. The full tag for a -CAT layer is determined by the form of the directly contained verb.

7.3   Verb sequences with HAVE and BE as catenative verbs

This section focuses on verb sequences with HAVE and BE as catenative verbs.

  • HAVE is used to form the perfect construction (see section 7.3.1)
  • BE is used to form either the progressive construction (see section 7.3.2) or the passive construction (see section 7.3.3)

Auxiliaries include forms such as will, have, and be. In a clause with a lexical verb, the presence of auxiliaries is optional. If present, they precede the main verb. Moreover, the different auxiliaries perform four different functions (see Table 7.2), and, when part of a tensed clause, these four different functions can join in sequences to allow twenty-four different combinations:

7.3.1   Perfect construction

The perfect form of a verb generally calls attention to the consequences of a prior event; for example, (7.14a) implies that he is still away, in contrast with (7.14b).

(7.14)
a.
He has gone to lunch.
b.
He went to lunch.

‘Had gone to lunch’ takes a past time point (i.e. when we arrived) as its reference point and is another way of establishing time relations in a text. The perfect tense is formed by:

  • turning the verb into its past participle inflection
  • adding a form of the verb HAVE before it.

This places multiple verbs in sequence, giving an IP-PPL-CAT layer in the annotation, as seen in (7.15) (= annotation for (7.14a)).

(7.15)
=N(" Mr. Yamada has gone to Tokyo . ")
"IP-MAT","NP-SBJ","N","Mr."
"IP-MAT","NP-SBJ","NPR","Yamada"
"IP-MAT","HVP;~cat_Ve","has"
"IP-MAT","IP-PPL-CAT","VVN","gone"
"IP-MAT","IP-PPL-CAT","PP-DIR","P-ROLE","to"
"IP-MAT","IP-PPL-CAT","PP-DIR","NP","NPR","Tokyo"
"IP-MAT","PU","."
"ID","1366_samples_kisonihongo@10,9"

The perfect construction can also be combined with the progressive (e.g. he has been going).

7.3.2   Progressive construction

The progressive (also known as the ‘continuous’) form of a verb generally describes events in progress. It is formed by combining the verb's present participle (e.g. watching) with a form of the verb BE (e.g. I was watching). The progressive can also be combined with the perfect (e.g. Bill has been watching).

7.3.3   Voice

Table 7.4: Tags for marking a logical subject

The ACTIVE VOICE is the basic, unmarked form of the clause.

The PASSIVE VOICE is the more marked form of the clause in which the -SBJ corresponds in meaning to an -OB1 or -OB2 (or very occasionally a -PRD) of a corresponding active clause.

The sentence It was eaten by our dog is the passive of Our dog ate it. A passive is recognisable from:

  • the past participle form eaten
  • the normal object (it) turned into the subject
  • the normal subject (our dog) turned into an optional preposition phrase with by
  • the verb BE, or some other verb, such as GET.
