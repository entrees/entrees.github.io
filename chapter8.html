7.1   Introduction

Codes of this chapter include codes that support the occurrence of existential there in section 8.1; and codes for main verbs that are phrasal verbs in section 8.2.


8.1   Codes for verbs that co-occur with existential there

This section introduces codes for verbs that co-occur with existential there (tagged EX):

  • [ex_V] (section 8.1.1),
  • [ex_Va] (section 8.1.2),
  • [ex_Vpr] (section 8.1.3),
  • [ex_Vp] (section 8.1.4),
  • [ex_cat_Ve] (section 8.1.5),
  • [ex_cat_Vg] (section 8.1.6), and
  • [ex_cat_Vt] (section 8.1.7).

8.1.1   [ex_V]

[ex_V] is the code to mark a verb that co-occurs with existential there with selection of an existential subject (-ESBJ) and nothing else. Example (8.1) illustrates how co-occuring there can be separated by interveing catenative verbs from the [ex_V] marked verb be with its existential subject trouble.

(8.1)
=N(" there <apos>s going to be trouble ")
"IML","EX","there"
"IML","BEP;~cat_Vg","<apos>s"
"IML","IP-PPL-CAT","VAG;~cat_Vt","going"
"IML","IP-PPL-CAT","IP-INF-CAT","TO","to"
"IML","IP-PPL-CAT","IP-INF-CAT","BE;~ex_V","be"
"IML","IP-PPL-CAT","IP-INF-CAT","NP-ESBJ","N","trouble"
"ID","20#587_christine_T33@12,14,2"

8.1.2   [ex_Va]

[ex_Va] is the code to mark a verb that co-occurs with existential there with selection of an existential subject (-ESBJ) + subject predicative that is an adjective phrase. Example (8.2) illustrates the [ex_Va] marked verb ai with selected existential subject nothing and subject predicative wrong with it.

(8.2)
=N(" there ai n<apos>t nothing wrong with it ")
"IP-MAT","EX","there"
"IP-MAT","VAI;~ex_Va","ai"
"IP-MAT","NEG","n<apos>t"
"IP-MAT","NP-ESBJ","Q","nothing"
"IP-MAT","ADJP-PRD2","ADJ","wrong"
"IP-MAT","ADJP-PRD2","PP","P-ROLE","with"
"IP-MAT","ADJP-PRD2","PP","NP","PRO","it"
"ID","72_christine_T14@4,8,11,2"

8.2   Phrasal verbs

In specific uses, a number of verbs include a preposition as a particle, for example, turn on, brighten up. These are often called phrasal verbs (or sometimes preposition verbs).

    The particle behaves as part of the verb, and when the verb has an object, this can occur before the particle, for example, turn the light on, brighten the room up, in contrast with PP dependents, for example, depend on the team, but not *depend the team on.

(8.8)
=N(" <ldquo> He <apos>s there <rdquo> <comma> he got out tersely <comma> curbing his rising excitement . ")
"IP-MAT","PUQ","<ldquo>"
"IP-MAT","IP-MAT-OB1","NP-SBJ","PRO","He"
"IP-MAT","IP-MAT-OB1","BEP;~Ip","<apos>s"
"IP-MAT","IP-MAT-OB1","ADVP-LOC","ADV","there"
"IP-MAT","PUQ","<rdquo>"
"IP-MAT","PU","<comma>"
"IP-MAT","NP-SBJ","PRO","he"
"IP-MAT","VBD;~phr_Vp.f","got"
"IP-MAT","RP","out"
"IP-MAT","ADVP-MNR","ADV","tersely"
"IP-MAT","PU","<comma>"
"IP-MAT","PP-SCON-ABS","IP-PPL","VAG;~Tn","curbing"
"IP-MAT","PP-SCON-ABS","IP-PPL","NP-OB1","NP-GENV","PRO","his"
"IP-MAT","PP-SCON-ABS","IP-PPL","NP-OB1","IP-PPL","VAG","rising"
"IP-MAT","PP-SCON-ABS","IP-PPL","NP-OB1","N","excitement"
"IP-MAT","PU","."
"ID","32_susanne_N14@20,22"

    Also, particles must precede an unstressed pronoun object, for example, turn it on but not *turn on it.
